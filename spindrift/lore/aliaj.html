unique biology & mating

A significant factor contributing to the small population size of the Aliaj, despite their generations in the Forge, is a statistically significant occurrence of infertility. Much like the liger, the biologies of the two origin species (human and Aliaj progenitor) appear to be only marginally compatible. This challenge is exacerbated by the fact that for fertile male Naski, pregnancy poses an extreme risk. Even for female Naski, pregnancy carries considerable danger, as the Aliaj fetus requires a gestation period exceeding one year and has an average birth weight more than double that of human infants. All Aliaj experience the stages of rut and heat regardless of their ability to produce offspring. For many Aliaj, these periods often represent the only times when sperm production is sufficiently potent and the Naski uterus sufficiently receptive to result in pregnancy, though around two-thirds of these conceptions result in spontaneous abortion.

Conversely, Aliaj are significantly more likely to conceive with humans than with one another. However, a profound biological preference for same-species mating often precludes such unions. Coupled with societal stigmas surrounding human-Aliaj relationships, these cross-species pairings are exceedingly rare.

Aliaj exhibit a strong compulsion toward monogamy, forming intense, often lifelong pair bonds, though exceptions exist, as with all living beings. For example, the connection between bonded Aliaj can weaken or dissolve due to hormonal changes within their bodies. Aliaj bonding is closely tied to the release of pheromones, which are secreted through a complex pathway resembling a fusion of human endocrine and lymphatic systems. This system produces immune fluids as well as hormones and pheromones, which are excreted through vesicular pathways roughly aligned with human lymph node locations.
